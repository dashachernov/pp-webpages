Overall, there are about 6 main methods of inserting a medical drug or substance into the body of a patient. Of course, there are many more, but the ones listed below are the most common ones.




(Image of an IV drip)

Intravenous
This is a very common and popular way of giving medicine, such as chemotherapy, place the medicine straight into the vein. It uses an intravenous (IV) pathway
How does this work?
Most likely, a nurse will place the IV needle and tube into the patient. A plastic needle is insurted into the patients lower arm - this causes some discomfort. Hanging on a stand, above, would be a plastic bag with liquid - this is the medicine. From the bag, through the tube and needle, the medicine should (without any problems) enter the bloodstream of the patient. Sometimes, however in very rare cases, a syringe may be used to push the medicine through the tube. Overall, this process (apart from the needle placement) is painless.
Analogies
Alternatively, a central line (a catheter) can be used - this is placed into patients who cannot use an IV or prefer to use a catheter. This is a tube that doctors would place into a large neck vein, groin, chest or arm to provide fluid and medication quickly. These can also be used to take blood tests. Using a central line may even be more convinient as IV tubes can cause irritation in the veins and they can also be very hard to place repeatedly. Short term catheters, these are placed in the patient's hospital room under local anesthesia and are removed before departing the hospital - they are placed for temporary access for providing drugs or medication. Long term catheters, these are inserted into the body for a very long period of time; by making a small incision, doctors will thread the IV line under the skin and into a vein to reach a second small incision a distance from the first (the distance will help prevent any future infection). Typically, these would be placed into the chest wall. Stitches would be made at both sites of the incisions and dressings (a plaster like covering with a large, clear window in the middle) would be frequently changed to also prevent infection.
Furthermore, a port can be placed to also access the vein - this is, in other words, a central venous cetheter - commonly known as PIC or PICC lines. Usually, a thin and flexible tube would be placed through a vein the patient's arm. Before this, however, a local anesthesia would be given to numb the space between the shoulder and elbow. After this, the doctor would thread a thin tube into the skin, through a vein in the patient's arm, so that it reaches the vena cava above the heart. The vena cave, or the superior vena cava, is one of the main veins in the venous system. This is sometimes a very long term device, hence it will need to be flushed (essentially washing it), but this will depend from case to case.


(Image of an oral pill)

Oral
This is a common method of inserting drugs into the body, many people have used this method and it remains a very popular one. Patients can take drugs, through this method, both at home or in hospital.
A pill, liquid or capsule is swallowed by the patient. However, understanding the drug dosage prior to taking the medicine is very important as it can lead to serious consequences. Moreover, sometimes if a repeated dosage intake it needed, the patient must understand the time invals between intakes.
Examples of oral drug treatment are: painkillers, blood cancer drugs (oral chemotheraphy has the same side effects as IV chemotherapy), vitamins.



(Image of an Injection)

Injection
There are two main/ common types of injection methods: intramuscular and subcutaneous.
Intramuscular
This injection is inserted into the muscle, for example: the arm, leg or buttock. Although the patient may experience a little "pinch", this should not be painful.
Subcutaneous
Differently to intramuscular injections, subcutaneous injections are inserted into the tissue just under the skin. Although the patient may experience a little "pinch", this should not be painful.



(Image of an intrathecal therapy treatment equipment)

Intrathecal Therapy
Some types of diseases spread through the nervous system. To treat such diseases, doctors commonly inject medicine into the spinal canal - this is much more effective for treating hard-to-reach cells in the spinal cord or brain cells.
How does this work?
In this case, doctors will do a lumbar puncture to insert a needle into the spinal cord. By removing spinal fluid, fluids containing chemotherapy or or medicine can be inserted. The removed fluid can be later examined.
Analogies
An Omaya can be placed under the patient's scalp. By doing so, the patient will no longer need a lumbar puncture and they can go home. Treatments will be provided through this device.


(Image of a rectal suppository)

Suppositories
Some type of drugs are best put into the body through the rectum - this includes many medical substances made for children.Suppostories are solid medications that are put into the body throught the rectum. This is used if a person cannot take medication through the mouth because they are having a seizures, unable to swallow oral medication, cannot keep oral medication in their system (they vomit) or they have an issue that blocks oral medications from entering the body. Other reasons for taking suppostories are if the oral form of a medical has a very bad taste, the patient has gastric problems (oral medication will not be effective) and many other reasons that depend on the patient. By putting medication through the rectum may also vary on the speed of adsorbtion and delivary - this is sometimes very important to understand and can also sometimes be preferred.
Typically recum suppostories are only an inch long and have a round tip at the end. They are commonly used to treat problems such as: constipation, hemorroids, fevers, anxiety (and other mental health probelms), pain and nausea.
To insert rectal suppostories, a medical professional does not need to be present, therefore this can be done at home. However, it should be mentioned that hygienic conditions should be maintained.



(Image with eye drops)

Dropping
This may be one of the most common methods, after oral. Eye drops, nose drops, ear drops; these all use drops to put drugs into the body. This is to treat areas that oral mehods cannot reach or those that are too small/ delicate to treat anyother way. Dropping can be done in the comforsts of home and medication may be bought without the permission from a doctor.
To do this method, although it can be done at home, hygienic conditions should be maintained.

 Ancient China.
At one point of time/ history, Ancient China was the leader of medical development. The first types of medical treatment in Ancient China, are thought to have started in around about 2000 B.C. (Before Christ), when an emperor who had started to look into the medical values of many different herbs. It has been heard that he may have tested many of his medical treatments on him self. His main recsources include: wood-bark, leaves, roots, flowers and other plant parts. This herbal medicine is still used in China, to this day. It must be outlined that a major factor of Ancient Chinese medical development was revolving around the, world-famous, Yin-Yang. This is a symbol consisting of two sides - balck and white. The black, the Yin, is thought to be associated with the dark or night; the white, the Ying, is associated with light and day. Both are needed to "balance" each other out. Ancient Chinese doctors, believed that when a person was sick, the balance within their body was lost and in order to treat the person, the balance must be restored by using medicine (e.g. herbal treatments). Another important factor upon which medicine was produce and/ or investigated was through the understanding of "chi". This is the energy of life. It was also said by doctors that when a person was sick, their life balance was unbalanced.

 19th Century
From the very start of the 19th century, pharmacy was built on top of discovries and innvovation made in the centuries prior. Diseases were concluded upon the syptoms they carried and doctors worked on the thought that the drug treating the disease should as strong as the disease. Bloodletting had fought its way to the popular charts of surgeon's treatments. Their method was to let the patient bleed until they faint and then give them enough calomel (mercurous chloride) for them to drool and consequently vomit. This was done to clean the stomach and bowels from toxins and poisons. Of course, this was showed to be inefficient due to its violence.

In the second half of the 19th century, there was the industrial revolution - this brought more innovation to medical equipment. Moreover, botanical and herbal treatments became popular once again. In 1860, cocaine was made from coca leaves; salicylic acid was extracted from willow bark in 1874 to make aspirin, quinine (a treatment for maleria) was extracted from China bark, however the subcomponent was not formulated until 1883. Nonetheless, no major breakthrough The century came to a close with two synthesised drugs that are very common in our world today: antipyrin (1883) and aspirin (1897). The creation of these two would later build the bases of, what we call, the Pharmaceutical Century. Furthermore, with more and more knowledge on microbial pathogens and vaccine technology, the first steps were taken to place pharmacy and medicine onto a scientific level or foundation.

 20th Century
In this moment in history, there were huge amounts of innovation happening, this is partly due to the improvement of communication between countries and scientists. Through computers, phone calls and international publications scientists were able to learn from each other and implement different techniques into their own pharmaceutical experiemnts. However, this teamwork and communication has made it harder to say what drugs or methods were made by whom because scientists did not work in isolation anymore.
Main advancements that were made were in fighting infection, improving our understanding of nutrition, endocrinology and other aspects.
World War 1 and World War 2 had given scientists new knowledge on cell biology and treatment. As well as new understandings on disease and virus treatment. This new knowledge meant that diagnostic tests and advanced treatments were created to improve therapies and body functions - this all happened in tight teamwork with biomedical engineers. Ultrasounds, nuclear magnetic resonance and computerized axial tomography were all created in the 20th century when doctors understood that lasers and other light techniques could be used to look into body tissue.
Germany played a big part in medical innovation in the 20th century. A German student found and did research on lead poisening. The same student did a lot of work with testing out different chemcials on the organisms of a diverse of diseases. Working with a fellow peer, the German student made progress into creating the world's first chemotherapy, this helped revolutionize control and treatment of disease.
The start of the penicillin era had started in this century.
It is very important to note that people in this century, the human's immune system started to be discovered. Edward Jenner, after many experiments, research papers and discussions, had introduced vaccination, specifically against smallpox. Over half a century was taken to produce vaccines that were safe and, mostly importantly, effective.
Finally, insulin was brought into the medical world. For about 30 years, scientists wondered about the causes and maybe possible treatments for diabtes. In 1889, two German scientists took dog pancreases to artifically make the disease. In 1901, an American scientist noticed the way of how cells work during diabetes. In further tests and experiments, especially in endocrinology, insulin was found.
As for the UK, in 1948, the NHS was created by health secretary - Aneurin Bevan. This gave people in the UK free healthcare and this brought more attention to community pharmacies because of the large amount of prescriptions.

    What are pharmaceutical sciences?

    Pharmaceutical science is when scientists study how to develop, manufacture and form new medical drugs or substances.

    What is Pharmacology?

    Pharmacology is the study of drugs/ other substances and their affects on a living organism. Moreover, if you look into clinical pharmacology, it is the study of drugs/ substances in treating a disease or illness (e.g. cancer).

    Who are pharmacists?

    These sciences are called pharmacists and they have the chance and power to transform an ill patient's life by creating a new treatment.

    Where do pharmacists work?

    Pharmacists can have many different job locations such as: part time healthcare workers, large supermarket emploi or retail pharmacy worker. They can also work for larger employers such as the NHS or elderly home carers. Furthermore, pharmacists can work in laboratories, designing new medical drugs or treatments for the public.

    What is the difference between pharmacology and pharmaceutical science?

    Pharmacology is specifically a branch in science where drugs (any man made, natural or endogenous substance) and its affect on a living organism are studied. On the other hand, pharmaceutical science is the study of the development and consumption of medical drugs/ substances - this links very closely to heath and chemistry.

    Why study these pharmacology and/ or pharmacy?

    In all, this degree would mean you would become a licensed health professional - this is always good to have. Furthemore, by working in the pharmacetical/ pharmacy sector, you would contribute to scientific innovations or make the lives of others better. Another important thing to note is that this career would be stable because the human health is always chnaging, new viruses, diseases or disorders are always being created; this means the work of pharmacy-pharmaceutical workers is never over. An like in any other scientific field, you would interact with people, most of which would share the same interests as you - this is always pleasent to have in a working environment.

    What qualifications would you need to pursue these careers?

    Firstly, in highschool you would need to major in biology and chemistry; many universities prefer you to also take mathematics or physics (however this is not demanded). Next, you would need to study an appropriate course in university, such as pharmacy or other. Some biomedical students sometimes transfer into pharmacology later in their studies, however this would need to be revised, depending on the university or educatinal institute.
